Etymology

The word r≈çnin is usually translated to 'drifter' or 'wanderer'; however, per kanji, r≈ç (Êµ™) means "wave" (as in body of water) or "unrestrained", while nin (‰∫∫) means "man" or "person". Status

According to the Bushido Shoshinshu (the "Code of the Warrior"), a samurai was supposed to commit seppuku (also harakiri, "belly cutting", a form of ritual suicide) upon the loss of his master.[6][7] One who chose not to honor the code was "on his own" and was meant to suffer great shame. During the Edo period, with the shogunate's rigid class system and laws, the number of r≈çnin greatly increased. Those r≈çnin who desired steady, legal employment became mercenaries that guarded trade caravans, or bodyguards for wealthy merchants. History
Graves of the forty-seven r≈çnin at Sengaku-ji

Until the Sengoku period, peasants accounted for the majority of daimy≈ç armies, so they accounted for the majority of ronin.[citation needed]

Especially in the Sengoku period, daimy≈ç needed additional fighting men, and even if a master had perished, his r≈çnin was able to serve new lords. In contrast to the later Edo period, the bond between the lord and the vassal was loose, and some vassals who were dissatisfied with their treatment left their masters and sought new lords. Many warriors served a succession of masters, and some even became daimy≈ç. As an example, T≈çd≈ç Takatora served ten lords. Additionally, the division of the population into classes had not yet taken place, so it was possible to change one's occupation from warrior to merchant or farmer, or the reverse. As Toyotomi Hideyoshi unified progressively more significant parts of the country, daimy≈ç found it unnecessary to recruit new soldiers. The Battle of Sekigahara in 1600 resulted in the confiscation or reduction of the fiefs of large numbers of daimy≈ç on the losing side; consequently, many samurai became r≈çnin. As many as a hundred thousand r≈çnin joined forces with Toyotomi Hideyori and fought at the Siege of Osaka. In the ensuing years of peace, there was less need to maintain expensive standing armies, and many surviving r≈çnin turned to farms or became townspeople. A few, such as Yamada Nagamasa, sought adventure overseas as mercenaries. Still, the majority lived in poverty as r≈çnin. Their number approached half a million under the third Tokugawa sh≈çgun Iemitsu.[citation needed]

Initially, the shogunate viewed them as dangerous and banished them from the cities or restricted the quarters where they could live. They also prohibited serving new masters. As r≈çnin found fewer options, they joined in the Keian Uprising of 1651. This forced the shogunate to rethink its policy. It relaxed restrictions on daimy≈ç inheritance, resulting in fewer confiscations of fiefs, and it permitted r≈çnin to join new masters.[citation needed]

Not having the status or power of employed samurai, r≈çnin were often disreputable and festive,[2] the group targeted humiliation or satire. It was undesirable to be a r≈çnin, as it meant being without a stipend or land. Bakin voluntarily became a r≈çnin, and eventually spent his time writing books (many of them about samurai) and engaging in festivities.[citation needed]
